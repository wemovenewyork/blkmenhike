Our Story
FOUNDER EST. 2024 HARRIMAN STATE PARK

Talon Wilson didn't set out to build a movement. He went for a hike at Harriman State Park, looked around, and noticed something that he couldn't unfind. He spent months looking for someone who had already done something about it. When he found no one, he started building.

It was a Saturday in early 2023. Talon had been hiking on his own for about a year — solo miles, mostly in the Hudson Valley, learning the trails out of Harriman on weekends when the city got heavy. He liked it for what it gave him: silence, effort, the particular clarity that comes from putting your body to work on terrain that doesn't care about your job title.

That Saturday he was four miles into the Suffern-Bear Mountain Trail when something made him stop and actually look. Not at the scenery. At the people. He stood there and counted. Dozens of hikers passing in both directions — families, couples, groups — and almost none of them looked like him. He could not remember the last time he had seen another Black man on a trail. Not a distant figure in the background. Not someone at the trailhead. On the trail, moving, present. He could not remember it because it had not happened.

"
It wasn't hostile. It wasn't a moment. It was just the architecture — built without us in the blueprint. And once you see that, you can't stop seeing it.

He finished the hike and drove back to Queens and sat with it. The absence wasn't new — he understood, logically, that Black Americans had been systematically excluded from outdoor spaces for generations. He knew the history. But knowing something and standing inside it are different. On the trail it stopped being history and became the present, and the present required a response.

He spent the next two months looking for one that already existed. He searched for communities of Black men who hiked together — not casually, not incidentally, but as a built thing with structure and intention. He found individual voices, scattered social media accounts, a few weekend groups with no real infrastructure. He found nothing that took the brotherhood seriously. Nothing that understood that for Black men, the trail is not just recreation. It is reclamation. And reclamation done alone is just a walk.

"
I wasn't looking to start something. I was looking for something that already existed. When I couldn't find it, I understood that meant I had to build it.

BLK MEN HIKE launched in 2024 with a single chapter in New York and thirteen brothers on the first group hike. Talon had spent months before that building the framework — not just organizing hikes, but thinking through what the structure needed to be. He wanted something with permanence. Something that would outlast any individual. A fraternal model, city by city, where men brought in other men, where the crossing marked a real threshold, where no brother hiked alone because the chapter wouldn't allow it.

Within six months there were five chapters. The framework he built in those early months — the crossing, the big/little mentorship structure, the chapter lead system, the pledge — scaled without breaking because it was built around a principle that doesn't require modification: men who move through difficult terrain together become something to each other that nothing else can replicate.

Talon still hikes every weekend. He still goes back to Harriman. He says the trail looks different now — not because it has changed, but because of what he knows is happening on trails across five cities on any given Saturday morning. Brothers moving. The line extending. The absence he stood inside in 2023 becoming, mile by mile, something else.
