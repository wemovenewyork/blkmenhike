FLAT CITY. DEEP TRAILS.

Houston doesn't have mountains, but don't sleep on it. The city is surrounded by piney woods, bayou systems, and the Big Thicket — one of North America's most biodiverse ecosystems. The trails here teach you a different kind of patience: navigating bottomland hardwoods, listening for wildlife, reading water. It's a different wilderness than the Rockies — quieter, stranger, and just as demanding.

HOU PRO TIP: Mosquitoes are year-round but brutal April–October. Bring DEET, wear long sleeves, and hit trails early morning. Winter months offer the best conditions — mild temps and minimal bugs.

Lone Star Hiking Trail
Sam Houston National Forest // 1 hr from HOU

The longest trail in Texas at 128 miles through Sam Houston National Forest. Day sections deliver dense pine forest, creek crossings, and the kind of quiet you can't find anywhere inside Houston. The Winters Bayou section is a standout — old-growth trees and serious solitude.

Brazos Bend State Park
Needville, TX // 50 min from HOU

Alligators. Hundreds of them. This is not a joke — Brazos Bend is one of the most wildlife-dense state parks in Texas. Trails wind through oak woodlands and along lakes teeming with gators, herons, and deer. Stay on the path, bring your phone camera, and respect the distance rule.

KNOW BEFORE
YOU GO.

TIP_01
FLAT DOESN'T MEAN EASY

Houston trails are low elevation but can be technical in wet conditions. Bottomland trails flood — always check recent trail reports before you go. Waterproof boots are a must.

TIP_02
WILDLIFE PROTOCOL

Gators, water moccasins, and wild hogs are real in the Houston area. Make noise on trail, give animals space, and never approach water's edge carelessly at dawn or dusk.

TIP_03
WINTER IS PEAK SEASON

November through March is the golden window — 60s temps, no humidity, minimal insects. This is when the chapter runs its longest distances. Lock in those dates early.
